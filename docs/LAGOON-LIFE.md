## Executive Summary

Lagoon Life is a biological lagoon treatment product for commercial swine operations. It combines Southland Organics' existing 500ppm Bacillus subtilis surfactin (BioSoap) with an anaerobic/facultative microbial package in a fulvic acid carrier to break down sludge, eliminate hydrogen sulfide odor, and reduce nutrient loading in swine waste lagoons.

The product is a reformulation of Revival (Liquid Lawn Aerator) — same histosol + BioSoap base, with the aerobic soil Bacillus blend replaced by lagoon-adapted anaerobic and facultative strains.

**The unique angle:** No competing lagoon treatment combines a high-concentration biological surfactin (500ppm) with targeted anaerobic biology in a single product. Most competitors use either chemical surfactants or naked bacteria with no penetration agent. Lagoon Life's surfactin breaks the biofilm so the biology can actually reach the waste.

### Why This Sequence Matters

Most lagoon treatments skip Step 1. They dump bacteria into a lagoon where years of compacted sludge sits under an impenetrable biofilm crust. The biology has no access to the waste it's supposed to digest. It floats on the surface, dies, and the customer re-orders with diminishing confidence.

**Surfactin is the key that opens the door.** At 500ppm — an extremely high concentration for a biological surfactant — it physically breaks apart the biofilm matrix, emulsifies the hydrophobic compounds that hold sludge together, and creates pathways for bacteria to colonize the waste layer.

Then the fulvic acid feeds and protects the biology as it moves into position.

Then the anaerobic/facultative strains do the actual work: digesting solids, neutralizing gases, processing nutrients.

**And then it becomes self-sustaining.** B. subtilis in the blend continues producing surfactin in situ. The biology maintains its own penetration agent. Initial dose breaks the crust; ongoing biological activity prevents it from re-forming.

## Microbial Package: Lagoon Blend

### Design Criteria

The lagoon environment is fundamentally different from soil (Revival) or poultry gut (BOB):

| Parameter | Soil (Revival) | Poultry Gut (BOB) | Swine Lagoon |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Oxygen | Aerobic | Aerobic/microaerobic | **Primarily anaerobic** |
| pH | 5.5–7.5 | 6.0–7.0 | **6.5–8.5 (variable)** |
| Temperature | Seasonal | 104°F body temp | **40–90°F seasonal** |
| Organic load | Moderate | High | **Extremely high** |
| Target waste | Compacted soil | Feed/pathogens | **Fats, proteins, cellulose, manure solids** |

Revival's indigenous 8-strain aerobic Bacillus consortium is optimized for soil. The lagoon needs **anaerobes and facultatives** that thrive in oxygen-depleted, high-organic-load conditions.

### Strain Selection Framework

**Facultative anaerobes** (work in both oxygen zones — surface and sludge):

| Strain | Function | Why This One |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| *Bacillus subtilis* | Surfactin production in situ, competitive exclusion of Salmonella/E. coli/Clostridium perfringens | Already in the existing portfolio. Produces its own biosurfactant — self-reinforcing mechanism. Naturally resistant to surfactin. |
| *Bacillus licheniformis* | Protease and lipase production — breaks down fats and proteins in sludge | Extremely thermotolerant, widely validated in commercial waste treatment. Handles the high organic load. |
| *Bacillus megaterium* | Waste processing enzymes, phosphate solubilization | Already in BOB consortium. Critical for phosphorus compliance — the nutrient that gets lagoons shut down. |

**Auxiliary facultatives** (if supplier has them in the same consortium):

| Strain | Function | Why This One |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Sulfide-oxidizing facultative Bacillus/Paenibacillus | Out-competes native sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRBs) that produce H2S | Attacks odor at the source — competitive exclusion of H2S producers rather than just oxidizing the output. |
| *Paenibacillus polymyxa* | Nitrogen cycle management, denitrification | Already in BOB consortium. Converts nitrate to N2 gas — removes nitrogen from the system entirely. |

### V1 Decision: Bacillus/Paenibacillus-Only Blend

**Keep the initial Lagoon Life blend Bacillus/Paenibacillus-only.** No Clostridium in v1. Rationale:

1. **Surfactin compatibility** — All Bacillus species are naturally resistant to surfactin (they produce it). Clostridium tolerance at 500ppm is unconfirmed and introduces a variable that doesn't need to exist at launch.
2. **Fermentation stays in current wheelhouse** — Bacillus spore-form production uses your existing infrastructure. Obligate anaerobe fermentation is a different process entirely.
3. **Shelf life simplicity** — Bacillus spores are shelf-stable in aerobic packaging. No need for nitrogen-purged headspace or special anaerobic packaging.
4. **Facultative Bacillus species handle both oxygen zones** — B. subtilis, B. licheniformis, B. megaterium, and B. pumilus all grow in anaerobic conditions. They're not obligate aerobes. The lagoon's anaerobic environment selects for their facultative metabolism naturally.

Clostridium or other obligate anaerobes can be evaluated for v2 after pilot data shows where the v1 blend falls short (if it does).

### Target CFU Spec (For Supplier Call)

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| **Concentrate strength** | Minimum 1×10¹⁰ CFU/mL, Bacillus-dominant, spore-form |
| **Finished goods (Lagoon Life)** | 10⁸–10⁹ CFU/mL (industry standard for lagoon bioaugmentation) |
| **Blend ratio** | ~0.1 gal concentrate per 1 gal finished product (same as Revival BOM) |
| **Form** | Spore concentrate — liquid or wettable powder dispersible into histosol/BioSoap carrier |

At 1×10¹⁰ CFU/mL in the supplied concentrate and 0.1 gal per 1 gal finished product, you're easily in the 10⁸–10⁹ CFU/mL range that commercial lagoon products target.

### Supplier Compatibility Confirmation

Request written confirmation that spores remain viable in:
- pH ~5–6 histosol environment
- 500ppm purified Bacillus-derived biosurfactant (surfactin)
- Mixed liquid carrier at ambient storage temperatures

### Open Formulation Questions

1. **Strain sourcing** — Does the culture supplier already sell or toll-produce anaerobic/facultative Bacillus blends for swine/dairy lagoons or manure pits? If yes, 60-day timeline. If custom blend required, get lead time and MOQ.

2. **Strain ratios** — In a lagoon, what's the priority balance between sulfide oxidation (odor), sludge digestion (capacity), and nutrient processing (compliance)? This likely varies by customer need. Start with a balanced blend; tune after pilot data.

3. **Supplier data** — Does the supplier have existing efficacy data (H2S reduction, sludge/TSS reduction, N/P changes) from lagoon or pit applications using their blend? This would accelerate your pilot narrative.

4. **Spore vs. vegetative** — Confirm whether spore-form concentrate germinates effectively in the histosol/BioSoap carrier or if it needs to be applied as-is and germinates in the lagoon environment.

## Dosing & Application

### Lagoon Size Reference

| Operation Size | Lagoon Volume | Surface Area |
|----------------|--------------|--------------|
| Small farrow-to-finish | 500K–1M gallons | 0.5–1 acre |
| Medium commercial | 1M–5M gallons | 1–3 acres |
| Large integrator (Smithfield) | 5M–15M gallons | 3–8 acres |

### Provisional Dosing Protocol

These are starting numbers anchored to market norms (~$0.50–$1.00/1,000 gal treated) and standard bioaugmentation CFU targets (10⁵–10⁶ CFU/mL in treated volume). Label as "pilot-calibrated" on any sell sheet.

**Shock Treatment (Initial / Problem Lagoons):**
- Purpose: Break biofilm crust, penetrate sludge, establish microbial colony
- Dose: **1 gallon Lagoon Life per 250,000 gallons** lagoon volume
- Application: Pour from lagoon edge or spray from bank using standard spray equipment
- Frequency: Single application, repeat if needed after 30-day assessment

**Maintenance Treatment (Monthly):**
- Purpose: Sustain microbial population, prevent biofilm re-formation, ongoing sludge digestion
- Dose: **1 gallon per 500,000–1,000,000 gallons** monthly, adjusted by odor/sludge response
- Application: Same edge-pour or spray method
- Frequency: Monthly

**Dosing by lagoon size:**

| Lagoon Volume | Shock Dose | Monthly Maintenance |
|---------------|-----------|---------------------|
| 1M gallons | 4 gal (~2×2.5G jugs) | 1–2 gal |
| 5M gallons | 20 gal (8×2.5G jugs) | 5–10 gal |
| 10M gallons | 40 gal (16×2.5G jugs or 1 drum) | 10–20 gal |

**Seasonal Considerations:**
- **Below 50°F:** Biological activity slows significantly. Increase dose or pause until spring. Southeast operations (GA, NC, SC) have a climate advantage — shorter cold windows.
- **Peak summer (80°F+):** Highest biological activity. Lightest dose needed. Natural biology is most active but so is H2S production — maintenance dosing keeps the beneficial population dominant.
- **Fall/Spring:** Optimal treatment windows. Biology is active, lagoon isn't thermally stratified.

### Application Methods

- **Edge pour:** Simplest. Walk the lagoon perimeter, pour measured amount at intervals. Surfactin disperses across surface.
- **Spray rig:** Pump sprayer or boom sprayer from bank. Better coverage for large lagoons.
- **Agitation system:** If the operation already has a lagoon agitator/mixer, dose directly into the agitation zone for maximum sludge contact.

## The Unique Angle — Summary

### What Makes Lagoon Life Different

**1. Surfactin as penetration agent (not chemical surfactants)**

Every competing lagoon treatment either uses chemical surfactants (sodium lauryl sulfate, ethoxylates) or no surfactant at all. Lagoon Life uses surfactin — a biological surfactant produced by Bacillus subtilis fermentation. It's one of the most powerful surfactants known, it's fully biological, and it's selectively antimicrobial (kills pathogens, spares beneficial Bacillus). No other lagoon product has this.

**2. 500ppm concentration**

This is extremely high for a biosurfactant product. Most commercial biosurfactant applications run 50–100ppm. Southland's BioSoap delivers 500ppm as standard. In a lagoon context, this concentration is what allows penetration into years of compacted sludge that lower concentrations can't touch.

**3. Self-sustaining biology**

After the initial shock dose, B. subtilis in the lagoon blend continues producing surfactin in situ. The biology manufactures its own penetration agent. Competing products require constant re-dosing of surfactant. Lagoon Life's biology is self-reinforcing — the surfactin breaks biofilm, the bacteria colonize, and the colonized bacteria produce more surfactin. Diminishing input, sustained output.

**4. Three-part mechanism in a single product**

Penetration (surfactin) + nutrition (fulvic acid) + digestion (anaerobic/facultative biology) in one application. Competitors typically address only one leg: either surfactant, or biology, or nutrition. Lagoon Life integrates all three because the base formulation (Revival) was already designed as a three-component system.

**5. BioSoap COGS is essentially zero**

Surfactin is a byproduct of existing Bacillus fermentation. It costs Southland nearly nothing to include at 500ppm. This means the single biggest technical differentiator adds virtually nothing to the cost of goods. Competitors would need to source or produce biosurfactants from scratch to match this.

**6. Organic / BioBased certification path**

BOB is already USDA Organic compliant (WSDA Materials Approved). PORT is already USDA Certified BioBased. Lagoon Life inherits the same ingredient profile — histosol, biosurfactant from bacterial fermentation, live cultures. The path to BioBased certification is straightforward. This is a corporate sustainability story for Smithfield's ESG team, not just a farm manager purchase.

**7. Built on proven formulation infrastructure**

This is not a new product from scratch. It's a microbial package swap on an existing, production-ready formulation (Revival). Same histosol supplier, same BioSoap supply, same packaging line, same bottling process. The only new element is the lagoon-adapted microbial concentrate.

## Risk Register

### 1. Surfactin Ecotoxicity at Discharge

Literature shows surfactin bioactivity against some aquatic organisms at low mg/L concentrations. While 500ppm in the product dilutes massively in a million-gallon lagoon (working concentration in the lagoon is orders of magnitude lower), and further dilutes before reaching any receiving waters:

**Action:** Prepare an internal technical note confirming that lagoon-diluted working concentration is below published ecotoxic thresholds. This will be asked by Smithfield's environmental team. Have the math ready.

### 2. ZeroPoint / Lagoon Life Interaction

You're selling HOCl biosecurity (ZeroPoint) AND live biology (Lagoon Life) to the same farm. Hypochlorous acid from barn washdown effluent flowing into the lagoon could kill your Lagoon Life inoculum.

**Action:** Develop operational scheduling guidance. Lagoon Life application should be timed so it's not immediately followed by heavy ZeroPoint barn washdown discharge. In practice, this means:
- Dose Lagoon Life on days when barn washdown effluent flow is lowest
- Allow 24–48 hours for bacterial colonization before significant HOCl discharge enters the lagoon
- HOCl degrades rapidly (hours) — the concern is acute exposure during initial colonization, not chronic interference

Include this guidance on the label and sell sheet. It's also a selling point: "We know our own products and how they interact — here's the operational protocol."

### 3. NC Attorney General / "Environmentally Superior Technology" Framing
