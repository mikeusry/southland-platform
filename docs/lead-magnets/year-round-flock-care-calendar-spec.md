## Design Brief

**Tone:** Friendly, knowledgeable poultry-keeper friend. Not corporate, not childish. Photo-supported but text-driven — this is a reference document, not a magazine.

**Layout principles:**
- Each page should work standalone if printed and posted in the coop
- Tables and checklists more important than long paragraphs
- One primary photo per page max, illustrative not decorative
- Footer on every page: page number, "southlandorganics.com/tools/flock-health-check/", small Southland logo
- Designed to be saved AND printed AND posted in feed shed

**Photo direction (Southland team to source):** real backyard chickens, not stock-imagery hens. Mixed breeds preferred (Buff Orpington, Easter Egger, Rhode Island Red feel — homesteader-coded). Dust-bathing, eating from feeders, in coop interiors. Avoid: cute studio shots, anything that looks like a children's book.

### Page 2 — The 12-Month Calendar

**Heading:** `Your 12-Month Flock Care Calendar`

**Intro paragraph (1 short sentence):**
> Most backyard flock issues are seasonal. Knowing what's coming each month lets you prevent problems before they appear.

**The table** (full-page, one row per month, color-banded by season):

**Spring (cream background)**

| Month | What's Happening | Care Routine | Watch For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **March** | Daylight returns, laying ramps up. Hens hungry after winter. | Switch to layer feed if you haven't. Probiotic 1–2× weekly. Inspect coop for winter damage. | Soft-shelled eggs (calcium need), broody hens, mites waking up |
| **April** | Mite season begins. New chicks arriving for many keepers. | First mite inspection. Set up dust-bathing area with sand + wood ash. Add ACV to water 1 week per month. | Feather loss around vent, blood on perches at night, pasty butt in any new chicks |
| **May** | Peak laying. Birds outdoors more. Predator activity high. | Maintain probiotic routine. Check coop ventilation as temps climb. Predator-proof any weak spots. | Reduced laying despite long days = stress check |

**Summer (light green background)**

| Month | What's Happening | Care Routine | Watch For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **June** | Heat stress risk climbs. Egg production may dip. | Frozen treats, electrolytes in water on 90°F+ days. Shaded run area mandatory. | Open-mouth panting, lethargy, fewer eggs |
| **July** | Mid-summer heat. Coop hygiene critical (flies, smell). | Probiotic 2× weekly during heat events. Refresh bedding more often. Refresh water 2× daily. | Pasty butt re-emerging in adults, foul coop odor, fly infestation |
| **August** | Late-summer mite peak. Coop conditions degrade fastest now. | Second mite inspection of the year. Deep coop clean. Continue probiotic 2× weekly. | Vent gleet (yellow discharge), pale combs (anemia from mites) |

**Fall (warm orange background)**

| Month | What's Happening | Care Routine | Watch For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **September** | Molt begins. Egg production drops naturally. | Boost protein (20%+ feed), continue probiotic, consider vitamin support. | Bare patches that aren't mite-related, lethargy that doesn't pass |
| **October** | Molt continues. Days shorten. Birds slow down. | Decide on supplemental coop lighting (or accept the laying break). Coop weatherproof check. | Bullying as flock dynamic shifts during molt |
| **November** | Pre-winter prep. Final deep clean before cold settles in. | Top up bedding (deep litter or fresh layer), block drafts but preserve ventilation, stock up on supplements. | Drafts on roost bars, frostbite-vulnerable single-comb breeds |

**Winter (cool blue-gray background)**

| Month | What's Happening | Care Routine | Watch For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **December** | Cold stress. Water freezing. Confined indoor time. | Daily water check (heated waterer if below 32°F). Probiotic 2× weekly. Vitamins for any stressed birds. | Frostbite on combs/wattles, dehydration, boredom-pecking |
| **January** | Coldest month. Lowest light, lowest energy. | Maintain routine. Don't open coop more than necessary. Probiotic + vitamins. | Sudden death in older birds (cold + age), behavioral changes |
| **February** | Pre-laying season approaches. Flock starts to wake up. | Probiotic + ACV rotation. Boost calcium ahead of laying ramp. Plan spring coop maintenance. | Soft-shelled first eggs of the season |

**Footer of page 2:**
```
Most years, sticking to this routine prevents 90% of issues.
```

### Page 5 — Coop Cleaning Checklist

**Heading:** `Your Coop Cleaning Schedule`

**Intro:**
> Cleanliness is the cheapest disease prevention you have. This is the schedule that keeps small flocks healthy with the least work.

**Four columns of checkboxes:**

**WEEKLY (10 minutes)**
- [ ] Refresh waterer with clean water
- [ ] Spot-clean droppings boards or heavy buildup
- [ ] Check feed for moisture or pests
- [ ] Visual inspection — count birds, check behavior, scan for limps or droopy posture
- [ ] Collect any forgotten eggs

**MONTHLY (30 minutes)**
- [ ] Replace or top up bedding
- [ ] Wipe down roosts with vinegar solution
- [ ] Check ventilation paths for blockage
- [ ] Mite spot-check (lift a few birds, check vent + under wings)
- [ ] Audit feed quality and date — toss anything stale

**QUARTERLY (2–3 hours)**
- [ ] Empty coop completely — birds out, all bedding removed
- [ ] Scrub roosts, walls, and corners with hot soapy water
- [ ] Inspect coop for damage (predator vulnerabilities, draft sources)
- [ ] Apply Desecticide as preventive (skip if recently treated)
- [ ] Sun-dry bedding materials before re-bedding
- [ ] Refresh nest box bedding entirely
- [ ] Check all hardware (latches, hinges, hardware cloth)
- [ ] Refresh dust-bathing area

**ANNUAL (half-day project)**
- [ ] Full structural assessment (rot, repairs, paint)
- [ ] Predator-proof audit (overhead, perimeter, dig-deterrent)
- [ ] Vet check or fecal float test if available

**Sidebar callout:**
```
THE 10-MINUTE WEEKLY HABIT IS THE WHOLE GAME

Backyard keepers who do the weekly checklist almost never deal with
emergencies. Backyard keepers who skip it almost always do.
```

### Page 6 — Stress Triggers + Mitigations

**Heading:** `Stress Is the #1 Trigger for Flock Health Issues`

**Intro:**
> Most flock health problems trace back to a stress event in the previous 7–14 days. Anticipating stress and supporting through it prevents most issues.

**Two-column reference card:**

| Stress Event | What to Do |
|---|---|
| **Adding new birds** | 30-day quarantine. Big Ole Bird in waterer for 14 days during integration. Watch for bullying first 72 hours. |
| **Predator scare** | Hen Helper + ACV daily for 5 days. Inspect every bird for puncture wounds (mortal until proven otherwise). Reinforce coop. |
| **Severe weather (heat or cold)** | Electrolytes in water. Catalyst vitamin support. Limit outdoor time during extremes. Expect laying drops. |
| **Feed brand or formula change** | Mix old + new feed 50/50 for 7 days, then 25/75 for 7 days. Probiotic during transition. |
| **Travel (you're away)** | Automated waterer + neighbor check-in. Probiotic 1× week before. Inspect every bird the day you return. |
| **Hen goes broody** | Mark her, decide hatch vs break. Move broody hens to separate brooder if hatching. Keep flock routine for everyone else. |
| **Molt** | Increase protein (20%+). Reduce expectations on egg production for 6–8 weeks. Vitamin support. Don't push. |
| **Adding chicks to existing flock** | NEVER mix until chicks are fully feathered (~6–8 weeks) and same size as adults. Quarantine each side, then "see but can't touch" introduction for 7+ days before merging. |

### Page 7 — When to Call a Vet

**Heading:** `When to Stop Self-Treating`

**Intro:**
> Most backyard flock issues respond to gut-health support, coop hygiene, and time. Some don't. These are the signs to escalate.

**Red flags list (with red accent or warning icons):**
- 🚨 Bloody droppings persisting more than 24 hours
- 🚨 Visible respiratory distress — gasping, gurgling, wheezing
- 🚨 Sudden death in two or more birds
- 🚨 Severe weight loss in any individual bird
- 🚨 Symptoms not improving after 14 days of consistent care plan
- 🚨 Neurological signs — twisted neck, seizures, balance loss
- 🚨 Coughing up blood, swollen face or eyes
- 🚨 Anything that scares you — your gut is right more often than not

**How to find a poultry vet (sidebar):**
```
POULTRY VETS ARE RARE. HERE'S HOW TO FIND ONE.

1. Your state extension office has a list — search "[your state]
   extension poultry"

2. Land-grant universities run poultry diagnostic labs that accept
   samples from backyard keepers (often free or low-cost)

3. The American Association of Avian Pathologists has a member
   directory at aaap.info

4. Some livestock vets will see chickens — call ahead. Many small-
   animal vets will not.

5. For non-emergency questions, your state extension specialist
   often answers email in 24–48 hours.
```
