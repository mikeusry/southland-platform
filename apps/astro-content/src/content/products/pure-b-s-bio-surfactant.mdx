## The Fly Is Not the Problem

Almost everything sold for drain flies targets the adult insect. That is the wrong target. Adults live about two weeks and are weak fliers that rarely leave the room they hatched in. Killing them changes nothing about the drain, and the drain is where the next generation is already developing.

A female drain fly lays up to 300 eggs in 48 hours. Larvae hatch in about two days and mature into adults in as little as eight. That is why a drain nobody thought about can go from invisible to a visible swarm in roughly two weeks — and why a spray that "worked" on Monday is followed by fresh flies by the weekend.

## Remove the Food Source and the Infestation Ends

Drain fly larvae eat biofilm. Take the biofilm away and there is nothing to eat, nowhere to lay eggs, and no next generation. That is the entire strategy, and it is the only one that holds.

The difficulty is that biofilm is genuinely hard to remove. It is not loose debris sitting in the trap — it is a structured matrix of bacteria, polysaccharides, and lipids adhered to the pipe wall, often well down the run of the pipe where no brush reaches. Hot water doesn't dissolve it. Bleach doesn't penetrate it. Caustic openers scour some of it away and leave a surface that regrows the film almost immediately.

A biosurfactant works differently. It attacks the bond between the film and the pipe, lowering the surface tension holding the two together until the biofilm releases and washes away in normal flow. The Bacillus bacteria that produce that surfactant stay behind and compete with the organisms that built the film, which is why treated drains stay clear longer than scoured ones.

## Treat the Right Drain

The most common reason a treatment appears to fail is that it was applied to the wrong drain. Drain flies stay close to where they hatched, but "the bathroom" can mean the sink, the shower, the floor drain, or the overflow.

Dry each suspect drain, then tape a piece of clear packing tape across the opening overnight, leaving a small gap for airflow. In the morning, the drain producing the flies will have them stuck to the tape. Treat that one — and, since biofilm rarely grows in only one place, treat the others while you are at it.

If you have treated every drain and flies keep appearing, check the sources people forget: the washing machine drain pan, the dishwasher air gap, the refrigerator drip tray, the HVAC condensate line, and the basement sump pit.

## Buying for a Facility

Commercial kitchens, food plants, and multi-site operations run a different version of this problem: floor drains and grease traps that are inspected, and drain flies that are a health-code finding rather than a nuisance. Dose those at 2–4 oz per drain weekly, and document the applications — a weekly treatment log is usually what a re-inspection wants to see.
