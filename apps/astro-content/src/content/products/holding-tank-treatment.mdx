## Why Ventilation Alone Isn't Enough

The U.S. Forest Service's "sweet smelling toilet" design uses a vent stack warmed by the sun to pull odor up and out of the building. It's clever engineering, and it works — when there's sun and wind. On a still, overcast day, or in a heavily forested site, the draft stalls and the odor has nowhere to go. That's when the green cloud settles in.

PORT solves the part ventilation can't: the chemistry inside the vault. By binding hydrogen sulfide and ammonia and shifting the vault's biology from anaerobic (odor-producing) to aerobic (odor-consuming), PORT removes the smell at its source. The vent moves air; PORT changes what's in it.

## Built for the Way Vaults Actually Get Managed

Vault toilets get pumped on a schedule and left to fill between visits. PORT is dosed to match that reality: add it to a freshly pumped vault, and the biology establishes and works on its own as the vault fills — no daily maintenance, no staff returning between pumps. For high-traffic weekends and holidays, a single gallon between cleanouts keeps the system ahead of the load.

## Trusted Where It's Hardest to Manage

National parks, U.S. Forest Service sites, and Bureau of Land Management facilities use PORT because remote public restrooms are the hardest sanitation problem there is: high use, no plumbing, no chemicals allowed near sensitive land, and a visitor experience riding on how the building smells. PORT is the biological, chemical-free answer that holds up under exactly those conditions — and it works the same in a backyard pit toilet or a construction-site portable unit.
