# Broiler Bill: Pains & Discomforts

O.K.  here you go:  My list of “Discomforts”  faced by those who venture into the world of contract commercial poultry farming in the U.S.

**Preface:** 

The National Future Farmers of America (FFA) Creed was written in the summer of 1928 by Professor Erwin Milton Tiffany, who was the chief teacher in the

Department of Agricultural Education at the University of Wisconsin.  This Creed is a testament to Agricultural life.  If you have never read it, stop now and take the time to do so, then read it again.

One part in particular, rings poignantly true for today’s poultry farmer.

***“I believe** that to live and work on a good farm, or to be engaged in other agricultural pursuits, is pleasant as well as challenging; for I know the joys and discomforts of agricultural life and hold an inborn fondness for those associations which, even in hours of discouragement, I cannot deny.”*

While the following list is about discomforts, know that I also experience the joys and hold an inborn fondness for those associations which, even in hours of discouragement, I cannot deny.

**Common “discomforts” experienced by contract poultry growers:**

- Does my work bring honor and glory to God?
- Does my work make this world a better place?
- Am I serving my Country through my work? A country that doesn’t control its food supply doesn’t control itself.
- Am I significant?
- Am I going to make it in this industry? Do I have what it takes? Emotional, Physical, Mental, Leadership, Communication, Self Discipline, Etc. What does it take? Where do I look to develop these skills specific to the world of Agriculture?
- Will I have enough money to pay bills?
- Am I saving enough for the rainy day that is coming?
- Will I ever be able to retire?
- Are my houses good enough to compete with the newer farms that are popping up all around? (By the way, they are always expanding, this is not a new pressure).
- Will my houses be able to stay competitive long enough to make the payment on the farm when they are older? This is a huge loan and the “new house” bonus goes away before the debt does
- Equipment maintenance: Repair or Replace? Upgrade or Stay with tried and true? Perform the work myself or hire it done? How much time should I spend on maintenance?
- Will “new” updated equipment be worth the investment?
- When will the company require that I update equipment, regardless of performance? How much of my money will they force me to spend?
- How do I balance family time and work time when the two intermingle so much? Life lessons on the farm…
- Should I hire extra help on the farm? How much do I pay? How do I manage the extra help? What jobs are they going to be responsible for? What jobs am I going to do? How to find good help? How do I protect my family from the help that I hire since they will be spending lots of time close to my home/farm? What do I do to keep them once I do find good help?
- Insurance: how much is enough? Really? How stinking much? I paid about $30K in insurance premiums last year for health, farm, home, auto, and life. Am I covered for that crazy accident? Or should I be even care and just risk it?
- Off farm income: How to balance the two? Which takes priority? How important is it? Our time is valuable. We only get one chance to raise our children, love our family, experience the world. “I am losing precious days. I am degenerating into a machine for making money. I am learning nothing in this trivial world of men. I must break away and get out into the mountains to learn the news” -John Muir.
- Climbing feed bins is dangerous. Dark, rainy, windy, cold, hot etc. all increase the danger.
- Dangers on the farm from tractors, truck, equipment, slips, trips, falls, etc.
- Dust…. Freaking Dust, Everywhere there is DUST! Wear a mask? Breath the dust? Spend ridiculous amounts of time to remove dust or let it build up? I know farms that clean like new every flock and farms that have never blown dust out in 20 years. Both of which perform well.
- Grass: It is a necessary evil. You can’t let just grow because it provides cover for rats, but it also is essential for erosion control and nutrient management. Mowing the steep banks around chicken house pads can be dangerous and time consuming.
- Smell- “smells like money to me” but to everyone else it still smells like chicken crap! The amount of showers and clothes washing is staggering. Even a quick pop your head in the door to check on the birds before church requires a change of clothes often.
- Noise: Fans, Trucks, backup alarms on equipment (that pitch seems to really travel), etc.
- Appearance of the farm: Certainly more important to some than others.
- Neighbors: Do they hate me? Are they going to sue me? How can I help them? Etc.
- Mud: I hate mud. Heavy trucks make mud. If they track it onto the road it becomes the an issue that can cause problems and friction between you and neighbors and possibly legal issues. Not to mention friction between the integrator and farm owner over who is at fault and responsible for clean up.
- Roads (I have almost 1.75 miles of gravel road to maintain plus a parking lot and loading area that totals approx 2 acres to maintain. Winter is hard on the roads with lots of big truck traffic.
- Lawsuits: The ever prevalent threat that seems to be more important to farms than ever before. I really wanted to help those people by serving as professional witness, but I was afraid of the repercussions We as an industry and a country must find a way to better share the world we live in.
- Company longevity: If the company has to shut down (Fire, disaster, bankruptcy ), my payments don’t stop.
- Contract requirements: Lack of control. No one likes to be told what to do on their own property, but that is the nature of business. As a contractor you must keep your one customer happy. Unfortunately, we have entered in a business where we have zero control over our contact prices and no ability to negotiate.
- Service techs:........Some are difficult, some are helpful. Some are knowledgeable, some just think they are…
- Fear of reprisal for speaking out, organizing, or publicly complaining about the integrator.
- Alarm systems: we really depend on them working.
- Generators: These are possibly the most important backup system on the farm, and one of the most easily overlooked systems.
- Disease: Everytime you open the door to the houses to step in, there is an uneasiness about what you might find.
- Medicines: Will we have any option if the birds are dying? Who determines?
- Mortality disposal: Freezers, Composting, Pits, Incinerators, etc. All with very specific maintenance schedules and requirements.
- Ventilation: Proper ventilation is crucial to bird health and performance. In the winter it is also crucial to controlling moisture in the litter. You must temper your ventilation so as not to have a very high gas bill that negates any performance gains.
- Lighting: I guess it’s just a stinking mystery…… there seems to be almost no consensus in the scientific community as to what works the best on broilers.
- Litter Management: Proper litter management can be the most time consuming expensive aspect to growing birds. It may also be the most important! $50k litter shed, $50k tractor, $25k decaker, $15k windrow machine, $3k leveling blade, $20k spreader truck, $50 skidsteer, $2k litter bucket; All for dealing chicken crap!
- Litter/Shavings: Timing of clean out so that you can sell litter and pay for some of the new shavings/bedding to go back in. $16,000 expense on my farm.
- Spreading/Selling Litter: More stink. If you want to maximise your profitability which is very important, you must market your litter. You must also do this under very strict guidelines and regulation.
- Security: For some reason people are curious about poultry farms and just like to drive up onto farms. Who knows if these people are up to no good. PETA/Vandals/Thieves
- Bio-security: This is a huge topic with so many points of concern I will reserve most for another list. Imagine that you are out to eat with your wife. Alarm goes off need to go back home. Not a major issue, but one that requires immediate attention. there are currently bio-security risks in your area. (L.T., A.I.) You must first go home. Change clothes and shoes. Quite possibly shower/disinfect. Drive a different vehicle to the chicken houses. Get stinky……. Then go home and repeat the process so you can please your wife who just left her dinner out on the town…..
- Cell phones/internet: Service for both are crucial for my farm controls and Alarm system. Rural America needs better access to high speed internet and better cell service.
- EPA/Local environmental regulations
- CAFO rules: WOW, STUPID CAFO RULES! Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations require specific rules. If a farm has over 125K broilers or 80k layers they must follow a CAFO guidelines and do annual inspections by a QCP which costs me about $600 and apply for the annual CAFO permit which is about $2500 but is often covered by the state.
- NRCS rules
- Professional organizations: Should I pay my dues? What will I get? Will the integrator think that I am joining a group that will harm them? (We have one in Alabama that integrator hate).
- Am I doing my part to tell the public about the importance of Agriculture as an industry?
- Am I considered a professional by the general public? Even though I manage a multi-million dollar business, I am viewed as “just a farmer” by society.
- How can I help dispel myths about poultry farming?
- How can I help do my part in providing the world with a safe affordable supply of protein? Am I?
- Why didn’t my neighbor tell me about that miracle potion that they have been running? Do they not want me to beat them? Are we in the same week? Have they been beating me? This competitive “Tournament” system is evil.
- Tournament system: If you must be part of a system that is competitive, then the playing field should be even. Chick quality is not equal. The integrators can and do track performance and they know that chicks from certain hens have a history of poor performance/liveability/weight etc. Feed is not always equal. I had a recent flock where none of my feed had antibiotics in it since I was in proximity to the mill where they need to ship from. Almost all the others had antibiotics in their feed.
- Why didn’t my service tech help me? Is he out to get me?
- Did the feed mill really deliver the proper amount of feed on that ticket? How is it possible that I got 6 truck loads of feed this week that weigh exactly 52,120 lbs………. Hint, It’s not.
- Feed outage: I have been out of feed for as long as 40hrs straight and a total 120 in one flock. It always seems to happen right at the end and wreck the feed conversion.
- I really hate cleaning up feed truck drivers feed spills.
- Fear of underweighting: I really thought those birds would weigh more than that when they processed…. so did the service tech, and the catchers, and the scales that I weighed samples on….. But I have no control over that. Their scales are right. ALWAYS! Hmmm. (I’m not a conspiracy theorist)
- Will that new magic liquid snake oil I read about on Facebook be worth the money?
- What are the other farms doing that make them place better than me?
- Why is it always my fault? It is according to the company.
- Underperforming cash flow prospectus: Companies often verbally assure growers that they will make much more money than they actually put in writing in the contract. In my case I can only earn about 60% of what they put in writing as the estimated cash flow, even if I have above average performance. I voluntarily entered into this agreement. I can voluntarily get out right? ( I knew going in that they lie, and I don’t want out)
- Taxes: OH BOY! WOW!, JUST WOW! Enough said

I am certain that I have left out many of the “discomforts” that some deal with and I may have listed some that other don’t mind.

All in all, if this list of “discomforts” ever starts to outweigh my list of “joys” then I will reevaluate.  Until that time, I choose to still  ***believe** that American agriculture can and will hold true to the best traditions of our national life and that I can exert an influence in my home and community which will stand solid for my part in that inspiring task.*
