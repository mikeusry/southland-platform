# Decision-Making Style

### **Core Decision Framework**

### **The Mental Equation**

Every decision runs through this filter:

1. **Will it hurt my birds?** (Immediate risk)
2. **What will the integrator think?** (Relationship risk)
3. **What are other top growers doing?** (Social proof)
4. **Can I afford it if it doesn't work?** (Financial risk)
5. **Is it worth the hassle to change?** (Effort vs. reward)

### **Decision Velocity**

- **Emergency Decisions** (disease outbreak): Hours - acts fast on vet/integrator advice
- **Operational Decisions** (new product): 2-3 flocks - tests carefully
- **Capital Decisions** (equipment): 6-12 months - researches thoroughly
- **Strategic Decisions** (expansion): 1-2 years - agonizes with family

### **Risk Tolerance Profile**

### **Risk Capacity by Category**

**Financial Risk Tolerance**

- **Micro** (<$500): "Try it on one house"
- **Small** ($500-2,000): "Half a flock test"
- **Medium** ($2,000-10,000): "Need proven ROI in 2 flocks"
- **Large** ($10,000-50,000): "Multiple neighbor success required"
- **Major** ($50,000+): "Only with integrator blessing/financing"

**Operational Risk Tolerance**

- **Will Risk**: 10% of birds maximum for any test
- **Won't Risk**: Feed/water/temperature basics
- **Test House**: Usually House 3 (not best, not worst)
- **Timing**: Only test during good weather/healthy flocks

**Reputational Risk Tolerance**

- **Zero Tolerance**: Anything that makes him look "weird" to peers
- **Low Tolerance**: Being first to try something new
- **Acceptable**: Being second or third adopter
- **Preferred**: "Several of us are trying it"

### **Risk Mitigation Requirements**

1. **Money-back guarantee** or return policy
2. **Local dealer** support (not just online)
3. **Gradual implementation** option
4. **Exit strategy** if it fails
5. **Peer backup** ("Jim will buy it off me if it doesn't work")

### **Innovation Adoption Pattern**

### **Adoption Curve Position: Late Majority (Specific)**

**The 2-3-10 Rule**

- **2 innovators** try it first (watches from distance)
- **3 early adopters** prove it works (starts asking questions)
- **10 peers** using successfully (ready to buy)

### **Innovation Categories & Timing**

**"Hell No" - Never Adopts**

- Unproven technology with no local support
- Anything requiring app-only interface
- Solutions from companies with no ag background
- Products that replace entire systems

**"Wait and See" - 3-5 Years**

- Automation technology (watches others debug)
- Genetic/breeding innovations
- Alternative energy solutions
- Precision agriculture tools

**"Cautiously Interested" - 1-2 Years**

- Biological/natural products with science
- Efficiency improvements with clear ROI
- Labor-saving devices
- Controller upgrades

**"Fast Follower" - 6 Months**

- Disease prevention with university trials
- Feed additives with integrator approval
- Safety improvements
- Proven mortality reducers

### **Adoption Triggers & Barriers**

**Accelerators** (Speeds Adoption)

- Integrator recommendation/requirement
- Disease pressure in area
- Three neighbors with proven success
- University trial at state school
- Can try with money-back guarantee

**Decelerators** (Slows Adoption)

- Requires training/certification
- Needs smartphone/computer
- Company less than 5 years old
- No local dealer/support
- Changes established routine

### **Data vs. Gut Decision Preference**

### **The 70/30 Gut Rule**

- **70% Gut**: Initial feeling about product/company
- **30% Data**: Validates gut feeling with numbers
- **Veto Power**: Gut can override any data

### **Information Processing Hierarchy**

**Level 1: Sensory** (Most Trusted)

- "I can see the birds responding"
- "Houses smell better"
- "Birds sound content"
- "Litter feels right"

**Level 2: Simple Metrics**

- Mortality percentage
- Feed conversion
- Average bird weight
- Water consumption

**Level 3: Peer Anecdotal**

- "Jim's mortality dropped 1%"
- "Tom's settlement improved $3,000"
- "Dave's birds looked better"

**Level 4: Company Data** (Skeptical)

- Trial results from company
- ROI calculators
- Testimonials from strangers

**Level 5: Complex Analytics** (Largely Ignored)

- Statistical significance
- Multi-variable analysis
- Predictive modeling

### **Data Presentation Preferences**

**Most Effective**:

- Before/after photos from local farms
- Simple bar charts showing improvement
- Cost per bird calculations
- Settlement check comparisons

**Less Effective**:

- Spreadsheets with multiple tabs
- Scientific papers
- Percentage improvements without context
- Industry-wide averages

### **Price vs. Quality Sensitivity Matrix**

### **Value Calculation Method**

**The Cents-Per-Bird Framework**

```
Value = (Expected Benefit ÷ Birds per Flock) - (Product Cost ÷ Birds per Flock)

```

- If positive by >$0.02/bird = Consider
- If positive by >$0.05/bird = Likely buy
- If positive by >$0.10/bird = Definitely buy

### **Price Sensitivity by Product Category**

**Price Insensitive** (Quality First)

- Vaccines/health products during outbreak
- Emergency replacement parts
- Chick quality
- Products preventing catastrophic loss

**Moderate Sensitivity** (Value Seeking)

- Preventive health products
- Litter amendments
- Cleaning products
- Routine medications

**High Sensitivity** (Price Shopping)

- Commodities (shavings, PLT)
- Standard equipment
- General supplies
- Non-critical improvements

### **Quality Indicators (Trust Signals)**

**Primary Quality Signals**:

1. Neighbor's settlement improved
2. Integrator uses/recommends
3. Made in USA/regional
4. Company rep knows chickens
5. Can visit operation using it

**Secondary Quality Signals**:

- University research backing
- Years in business
- Professional packaging
- Local inventory available
- Technical support hotline

### **Influence Network Architecture**

### **Decision Influencers (Ranked)**

**Tier 1: Veto Power**

- **Integrator field tech**: Can kill any decision
- **Wife**: Financial decisions over $5,000
- **Top grower in complex**: Sets the standard

**Tier 2: Strong Influence**

- **Coffee shop crew** (3-4 trusted growers)
- **Feed store owner** (if old-timer)
- **Service tech** (equipment decisions)
- **Veterinarian** (health products)

**Tier 3: Moderate Influence**

- **County extension agent**
- **Sales reps** (if long relationship)
- **Online grower forums** (validating only)
- **Kids** (technology decisions)

**Tier 4: Weak Influence**

- **Trade publications**
- **Company marketing**
- **Social media ads**
- **Webinars/podcasts**

### **Decision Timing Patterns**

### **Seasonal Decision Windows**

**High Receptivity Periods**:

- **Between flocks** (clean-out): Capital improvements
- **Winter downtime**: Planning/research mode
- **Post-settlement** (good check): Reward purchases
- **Pre-placement**: Preventive products

**Low Receptivity Periods**:

- **Week 1-2** (brooding): Too critical
- **Week 4-5** (max growth): Too busy
- **Disease outbreak**: Emergency only
- **Bad settlement**: Closed wallet

### **Daily Decision Windows**

- **Morning coffee** (5-6 AM): Most open-minded
- **Post-lunch** (1-2 PM): Quick decisions
- **Evening** (7-8 PM): Research mode
- **During crisis**: Survival mode only

### **Post-Decision Behavior**

### **Buyer's Remorse Pattern**

- **Day 1-7**: Second-guessing, hypersensitive
- **Day 8-30**: Looking for validation
- **Day 31-60**: Preliminary judgment
- **Day 61+**: Fixed opinion formed

### **Success Sharing Behavior**

- **Small Win**: Mentions at coffee shop
- **Medium Win**: Posts on Facebook
- **Big Win**: Brings people to see
- **Massive Win**: Becomes evangelist

### **Failure Response Pattern**

1. Blames application/timing first
2. Questions if followed directions
3. Considers external factors
4. Rarely admits product failure
5. Never admits decision error publicly

### **Decision Script Examples**

**Considering New Product**: *"Well, I seen Jim's birds after he used it, and they did look better. The integrator tech didn't say not to, and Tom's been using it for two flocks now. At $0.03 per bird, I reckon I could try it on House 3 and see. If it works like they say, that'd pay for itself in lower mortality. But I ain't changing everything at once - just gonna test it small first."*

**Rejecting New Product**: *"I appreciate you coming by, but I ain't heard nobody around here using that. Seems complicated, and I got a system that works. Maybe after some other boys try it and I see their settlements improve. You got any neighbors using it I could talk to?"*

### **Implications for Sales/Marketing**

1. **Lead with peer success** stories, not features
2. **Provide simple ROI** in cents-per-bird
3. **Offer small trial** sizes/areas first
4. **Time outreach** to decision windows
5. **Build relationship** before selling
6. **Never pressure** - let him percolate
7. **Use local proof** over distant data
8. **Respect the integrator** relationship
9. **Make wife feel comfortable** with investment
10. **Follow up post-purchase** for validation

This decision-making framework shows Bill as a cautious but rational operator who relies heavily on social proof and gradual testing, with deep respect for established relationships and proven results over promises.
